Butylated Hydroxyanisole (BHA)

RESTRICTED AGENT Class: Synthetic Antioxidant

Compound Definition

Butylated Hydroxyanisole (BHA) is a synthetic solid waxy white or yellowish substance. It is a mixture of 2-tert-butyl-4-hydroxyanisole and 3-tert-butyl-4-hydroxyanisole. It is primarily used as a preservative to prevent fats and oils in food from becoming rancid (oxidizing).

Industrial Function

Manufacturers utilize BHA to extend shelf life in fat-rich dry kibbles. It is chemically stable at high temperatures, making it cheaper and more effective for extrusion processing than natural alternatives like Mixed Tocopherols (Vitamin E) or Rosemary Extract.

Toxicology and Risk Profile

Mechanism of Action:

BHA metabolites (tert-butylquinone) induce hyperplasia and cytotoxicity in the gastric epithelium. Long-term exposure stimulates cell proliferation, which acts as a tumor promoter in rodent models.

Key Citations:
  • Ito, N. et al. (1986). Carcinogenicity and modification of the carcinogenic response by BHA. Cancer Research.
  • IARC Working Group (1986). Evaluated as "Possibly carcinogenic to humans (Group 2B)".
  • Botterweck, A.A. et al. (2000). Intake of BHA and stomach cancer risk. Food Chem Toxicology.

Registry Guidance

Valid, non-synthetic alternatives exist (Tocopherols). The presence of BHA indicates a cost-optimization decision over a safety-optimization decision.
